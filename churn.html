This project dives into the big challenge of customer churn in the telecom world. My main goal was to build a smart model that not only predicts which customers might leave but also helps us understand *why* they're leaving or staying. This way, we can create better strategies to keep them around. I used the "Telco customer churn: IBM dataset," which is a public dataset available on Kaggle.

View Project Code on GitHub

Project Overview

For any company with subscriptions, knowing why customers cancel (churn) is super important for future revenue and growth. This project tackles that head-on by building a solid prediction model for customer churn in a telecom setting. I wanted to do more than just accurately forecast churn; I aimed to pinpoint the key reasons customers stick around or leave, so we can develop targeted plans to help them stay.

Methodology

My project followed a few key steps:

  1. Data Preparation: First, I cleaned up the data, filled in any missing bits, and made sure all the data types were correct.
  2. Feature Engineering: This was a crucial part where I turned existing data into more useful insights. This included:
    • Grouping continuous variables: For example, categorizing customer ages (like '25-39', '40-54') and how long they've been a customer (like 'under 1 year', '1-3 years').
    • Creating new features: I developed things like a Services_Count (how many core services they use) and an interaction term to see how, say, a customer's time with us affects those on a month-to-month contract.
  3. Model Development: I built a Logistic Regression model to predict churn. I chose this model because it's great for understanding *why* certain predictions are made.
  4. Feature Importance Analysis: After building the model, I dug into its results to clearly see which factors had the biggest impact on customers churning or staying.

Key Findings & Outcome

The Logistic Regression model I developed turned out to be really effective, hitting an impressive 98.5% overall accuracy. It was particularly good at identifying customers who would churn, with 100% precision (meaning almost every customer it predicted would leave actually did) and 98% recall (it caught almost all of the customers who actually churned).

Key Factors Driving Customer Churn:

  • Month-to-Month Contracts: Customers on these contracts are highly likely to churn, and this risk actually grows the longer they stay on this flexible plan.
  • High Monthly Charges: A strong sign that a customer might leave, possibly because they feel they're paying too much or not getting enough value.
  • Lack of Tech Support: Customers without technical support are significantly more likely to churn, showing how important good customer help is.
  • New Customers (Under 1 Year Tenure): This group naturally has a higher churn risk, so it's vital to engage with them proactively early on.

Key Factors Driving Customer Retention:

  • Tech Support Subscriptions: Customers who pay for technical support tend to stick around much longer.
  • Longer-Term Contracts (One-Year/Two-Year): These contracts offer a lot of stability and really help reduce churn compared to month-to-month options.
  • Mid-to-Long Term Tenure: Customers who've been with us for 1-3 years or more show strong loyalty and are less likely to churn over time.

Additional Finding: The Internet Service Puzzle

Beyond the main churn drivers, I found something interesting about Internet Service Type.

While customers who *only* have our phone service (no internet through us) are more likely to stay, those who *do* have our internet service (both Fiber Optic and DSL) actually show a slightly increased risk of churning. This is a bit unexpected! It suggests that these customers might be finding better or cheaper internet options elsewhere, making our internet service a key area to look at for competitive analysis and to re-evaluate its value to customers.

Recommendations

Based on these insights, I'd suggest the telecom company:

  • Focus on Month-to-Month Customers: Start proactive campaigns to retain month-to-month customers, especially once they hit the 6-12 month mark. Highlight the benefits of longer contracts and offer special incentives.
  • Boost Tech Support & Contract Sales: More actively promote the value of Tech Support and the advantages of one-year and two-year contract options.
  • Investigate Internet Service: Dig deeper into customer satisfaction with current internet services and compare our offerings with competitors' pricing and reliability. If internet service is indeed a churn trigger, it likely points to external market pressures or perceived service gaps.
