Project Overview

This project dives into the effectiveness of three different promotional strategies used by a fast-food chain, all tested through an A/B/C methodology. We used the "Fast Food Marketing Campaign A/B Test" dataset, which is available on Kaggle.

View Project Code on GitHub

Project Objectives

  • Assess Significance: See if the total sales performance among the three promotions shows a statistically meaningful difference.
  • Identify Top Performers: Figure out which promotions worked best, and which didn't.
  • Actionable Advice: Provide clear, data-driven recommendations for the fast-food chain's future promotions.

Dataset

The analysis uses simulated sales data from a multi-week promotional campaign across various fast-food store locations. Key variables include:

  • MarketID and LocationID: Unique codes for markets and individual stores.
  • MarketSize: How big the market is (Small, Medium, Large).
  • AgeOfStore: How old the store is in years.
  • Promotion: Which promotion the store received (1, 2, or 3).
  • SalesInThousands: Weekly sales figures, which I grouped into Total Sales per Location to measure the main outcome.

Methodology

  • Data Preprocessing: I combined weekly sales data into Total Sales per Location, treating each store as a single unit for the experiment.
  • Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA): I used visualizations like box plots to understand how sales were distributed across different promotions. I also checked MarketSize and AgeOfStore to ensure the groups were balanced for fair comparisons.
  • Statistical Hypothesis Testing:
    • ANOVA (Analysis of Variance): I ran an ANOVA test to see if there was a statistically significant difference in average total sales across the three promotions.
    • Tukey's HSD Post-Hoc Test: Since ANOVA showed an overall difference, I used Tukey's Honestly Significant Difference (HSD) test for detailed, pairwise comparisons between promotions, making sure to control for overall error.

Key Findings

The statistical analysis revealed clear differences in promotional effectiveness:

  • Overall Impact: The ANOVA test confirmed that the choice of promotion significantly impacts total sales (p-value < 0.05).
  • Promotion 2 Underperformed: Promotion 1 brought in about $43,078 more in sales per location than Promotion 2 (a statistically significant difference, p < 0.05). Promotion 3 also outperformed Promotion 2 by roughly $32,140 (p < 0.05).
  • Promotions 1 & 3 Similar: There was no significant difference in total sales between Promotion 1 and Promotion 3 (p > 0.05); both performed equally well.

Recommendations

Based on these findings, I'd suggest the fast-food company:

  • Drop Promotion 2: This promotion consistently resulted in much lower sales, so it should be stopped for future campaigns.
  • Prioritize Promotions 1 and 3: Both are effective and statistically similar in driving sales. The final choice between them can depend on other factors like cost or how easy they are to run.

Limitations

  • Missing Cost Data: We couldn't do a full ROI analysis without cost data for each promotion.
  • Short Study Period: The 4-week sales data limits our understanding of long-term effects.
  • Uncontrolled External Factors: Things like local events or competitor actions weren't accounted for.
  • Market Size Imbalance: A small imbalance in MarketSize across groups might slightly affect the results.
  • Limited Generalizability: These findings might not perfectly apply to all stores or future campaigns without more testing.

Future Work

To enhance this analysis and provide more robust recommendations, consider:

  • Cost-Benefit Analysis: Add promotional cost data to calculate the Return on Investment (ROI) for each strategy.
  • Longer-Term Tests: Run A/B tests for longer (e.g., 8-12 weeks) to see sustained impact and customer behavior.
  • Advanced Modeling: Use techniques like ANCOVA or mixed-effects models to better control for other variables.
  • Qualitative Feedback: Gather insights from store managers and customers about operational challenges and promotion appeal.
  • Targeted A/B Test (P1 vs. P3): If needed, run a larger A/B test specifically comparing Promotion 1 and Promotion 3 to find subtle differences.
